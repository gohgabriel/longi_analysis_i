1.2 The APCES Framework

Architecture, Parameters, Criterion, Estimator, Solver

Learning objectives
  • Decompose any statistical method into its five constituent components
  • Understand what each component contributes and why it matters
  • Apply the APCES lens to OLS as a worked example
Notation $\theta_{\text{true}}$ is read "theta true", standing for the real population value which we can never directly observe. $\hat\theta$ ("theta hat") is our estimate from the sample. The hat is a reminder that it is a best guess, not the truth.
Key concept The DGP is not a description of reality — it is a deliberate simplification. A good DGP captures the features of the world that matter most for your research question.
Remember Parameters live in the population. Estimates live in your sample. This distinction is the entire foundation of inferential statistics.
ML connection In machine learning, the criterion is called a loss function. OLS's RSS is equivalent to mean squared error (MSE) — the most common loss in regression.
Gauss–Markov theorem When the DGP assumptions hold, OLS is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE) — no other linear unbiased estimator has lower variance. This is the theorem that justifies why OLS is widely used for cross-sectional data.
Coming up The Bayesian chapter uses MCMC (Markov Chain Monte Carlo) as its solver — a stochastic algorithm that explores the full posterior distribution by drawing samples from it, rather than optimising toward a single answer.

Before looking at specific analysis methods, it helps to have a unified lens for understanding what any method is actually doing. We describe that lens here: every statistical method can be decomposed into five components — Architecture, Parameters, Criterion, Estimator, Solver.

1. Architecture

When evaluating a program, we typically start with a research question:

RQ: After receiving the intervention, how much will its recipients improve?

What we are actually saying is that, in the general population, there is some ground truth — a number out there that perfectly quantifies this effect. We call this $\theta_{\text{true}}$.

The catch is that $\theta_{\text{true}}$ is never directly observable. All we ever have access to is data from our sample, and we work backwards from that data to make our best guess. In this sense, data analysis is always a reverse-engineering exercise.

Any number of different $\theta_{\text{true}}$ values could have plausibly generated the data we observe. Without further constraints, the problem is hopelessly underdetermined. This is where assumptions come in. By making explicit claims about how $\theta_{\text{true}}$ generates data, we dramatically narrow the search space and make the problem tractable.

We call these assumptions the data-generating process (DGP). In a perfect world, every child who receives the program would improve by exactly the same amount. But in reality some improve by 2 points, others by 6. There is individual variability around the true average effect, and our assumptions need to account for that. We write them down as an equation:

$$y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{time} + \epsilon$$

A child's developmental score ($y$) is determined by some baseline level ($\beta_0$), plus however much they improve over time ($\beta_1$), plus leftover noise ($\epsilon$). We further assume that this noise averages to zero and has some characteristic spread $\sigma^2$:

$$\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

Together, these two lines are your working theory of how the world produced the data in front of you.

Formally
The architecture defines a family of probability distributions $p(y \mid \theta)$, parameterised by $\theta_{\text{true}}$. Different architectural choices — what predicts what, how errors behave, whether observations are independent — lead to entirely different estimation strategies.

2. Parameters

Once we have written down our DGP, the next question is: what exactly are we trying to estimate? These are the parameters — the specific unknowns that, once pinned down, fully describe how the world is generating our data.

Reality is very complex — a child's developmental trajectory is shaped by countless factors. Parameterisation is the act of boiling all of that complexity down into just a few key numbers that capture the most important features of the story.

In our example, the parameters fall out naturally from the equation. We want to know $\beta_1$ — the average improvement attributable to the program — and $\sigma^2$ — how widely individual children vary around that average. Both are unknown; both need to be estimated from data.

This points to a distinction worth holding onto. There is the true parameter value in the population — $\theta_{\text{true}}$ — which we can never directly observe. And there is our estimated value, $\hat{\theta}$ ("theta hat"), calculated from our sample. The hat is a reminder that this is our best guess, not the truth itself.

In practice, parameters come in a few flavours:

  • Point estimates — our best single guess at each unknown (e.g. "the program improves scores by 4.2 points on average")
  • Uncertainty estimates — how much that guess would vary across different samples (e.g. the standard error of $\hat{\beta}_1$)
  • Covariance structure — how our estimates relate to one another (e.g. does a higher baseline $\hat{\beta}_0$ tend to go with a smaller estimated effect $\hat{\beta}_1$?)

3. Criterion

We now know which parameters we want to estimate. But there are many possible values of $\hat{\beta}_1$ that could plausibly describe our data. How do we decide which one to go with? We need a rule — known as the criterion — that defines what "good" estimation looks like.

Think of it like a scoring system. For any candidate value of $\hat{\beta}_1$, the criterion asks: how well does this estimate fit the data we collected? (In machine learning, this is also called the loss function.)

A very common criterion is the one used by Ordinary Least Squares (OLS). If our model predicts every child should improve by 4 points but some improved by 2 and others by 6, there are residuals — discrepancies between predictions and observations. OLS says: find the $\hat{\beta}_1$ that makes those residuals as small as possible. Formally:

$$\text{RSS} = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$$

The criterion is the bridge between your architectural assumptions and the actual estimation procedure. Change the criterion, and you change what your estimates are optimising for — which is why different methods can give different answers even on the same data.

4. Estimator

The criterion tells us what we are optimising for. The estimator is the actual rule — a formula — that takes our data and produces a concrete value for $\hat{\beta}_1$. For OLS:

$$\hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$$

Don't worry about the mechanics. The important thing is what it represents: a precise, reproducible recipe that maps raw data to parameter estimates.

A good estimator comes with theoretical guarantees — mathematical proofs about how estimates behave across many hypothetical repeated samples. The key properties are:

  • Unbiasedness — on average, across many samples, the estimate lands on the truth. We might be off in any single sample, but we are not systematically wrong in any direction.
  • Consistency — as sample size grows, the estimate converges to $\theta_{\text{true}}$. More data means we get closer to the truth.
  • Efficiency — among all unbiased estimators, ours has the smallest variability across samples. It is not just right on average — it is as precise as possible.

These guarantees only hold when certain assumptions about the DGP are met:

  • Correct specification — the functional form of the model actually reflects reality (e.g. the relationship is genuinely linear).
  • Exogeneity — predictors are uncorrelated with the error term; no selection bias is baked into the data.
  • Independence — one observation's residual tells us nothing about another's.
  • Identical distribution — all errors are drawn from the same $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$; no subject is systematically noisier than another.
Watch out
Longitudinal data almost always violates the independence and identical distribution assumptions — measurements on the same person over time are correlated by design. This is why standard OLS is typically not appropriate for panel data, and why most of this course is dedicated to methods that relax these constraints.

5. Solver

Think of everything we have built so far as a maze. The architecture and parameters define the maze itself. The criterion tells you how far you are from the exit at any given point. The estimator, when its assumptions are met, guarantees that an exit actually exists.

But none of that tells you how to find it.

That is the job of the solver — the computational procedure that navigates the maze and finds the parameter values satisfying your criterion. In simple cases there is a direct path, calculable in one step. In more complex cases you have to feel your way through iteratively.

In our OLS example, we are lucky — there is a clean analytical solution. Given the RSS criterion, a closed-form formula jumps straight to the answer:

$$(X^\top X)\hat{\beta} = X^\top y$$

No iteration required. But as models grow more complex, closed-form solutions become unavailable, and solvers have to work harder. Common strategies include:

  • Iterative algorithms — start with a guess, compute how far you are from the exit, take a step in the right direction, repeat until convergence (e.g. Newton-Raphson, EM algorithm).
  • Stochastic sampling — rather than finding a single exit, explore the entire maze probabilistically and map out all the plausible regions (e.g. MCMC, used in Bayesian inference).

The solver is the most invisible of the five components. It runs quietly in the background every time you hit "run" and you never think about it. But as you (may?) learn, once you use more complex methods, sometimes the default solvers that the software uses will break! Solvers can fail to converge, get stuck, or give you answers that look fine but are quietly wrong.

Summary: APCES applied to OLS

Component What it means OLS
Architecture Assumptions about how the world generates data $y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \cdot \text{time} + \epsilon$, with $\epsilon \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. Errors are independent, identically distributed, and homoscedastic.
Parameters The unknown quantities to estimate $\beta_0$ (baseline), $\beta_1$ (treatment effect), $\sigma^2$ (spread); estimated as $\hat{\beta}_0$, $\hat{\beta}_1$, $\hat{\sigma}^2$
Criterion The scoring rule that defines "good" estimation Minimise RSS: $\sum(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$
Estimator The formula that maps data to estimates $\hat{\beta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$. Under Gauss-Markov, this is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE).
Solver The computational procedure that finds the answer Analytical: normal equations $(X^\top X)\hat{\beta} = X^\top y$, solved directly. No iteration required.

The APCES framework is a neutral lens — it can be applied to any statistical method, regardless of philosophical tradition. But the five components do not tell you how to interpret your estimates once you have them. That is where statistical philosophy comes in, and there are three major traditions that answer this question differently:

  • Frequentism, which treats parameters as fixed unknowns and uses repeated-sampling logic to reason about uncertainty
  • Bayesianism, which treats unknown parameters as probability distributions and updates them in light of data
  • Modern ML / empiricism, which largely sidesteps inference and focuses on predictive performance instead

The OLS example we walked through in this chapter sits squarely in the frequentist tradition. In the next section, we dive deeper into what that means.
