What is Bayesian inference?

Combining prior knowledge with observed data

Frequentist view Frequentists only extract the peak of the likelihood (the MLE) and discard the rest — because treating θ as a random variable is philosophically off-limits.
Philosophical note Bayesians reject sampling distributions entirely: with only one real experiment, hypothetical replications are metaphysical fiction.
Special case For normal models with known variance, the sampling distribution and the likelihood are identical — frequentists and Bayesians literally look at the same curve.
Uninformative prior Set σ₀ to a very large value (e.g. 20). The prior flattens out and the posterior converges on the likelihood — recovering the frequentist result.
Prior choices Common shapes: normal (symmetric belief), half-Cauchy (scale parameters that must be positive), or t-distribution (heavier tails = more scepticism about extreme values).
Precision weighting Posterior mean = (prior precision × prior mean + data precision × observed mean) / total precision. The sharper distribution wins.
HDI vs CI A 95% credible interval is a direct probability statement: P(θ ∈ HDI | data) = 0.95. A frequentist 95% CI makes no such claim about the parameter — only about the procedure.
Learning objectives
  • Understand why frequentist inference alone is insufficient when prior knowledge exists
  • Distinguish the likelihood, prior, and posterior distributions
  • Interpret Bayes’ theorem as a principled way to update beliefs with data
  • Understand how the strength of the prior relative to the data shapes the posterior

In the frequentist section, we saw that while θtrue is (assumed to be) fixed, we can still construct and approximate a sampling distribution around it to perform statistical inference. However, this is a purely data-driven approach — the plausible values of θ are derived entirely from your data.

In practice, we rarely arrive at a study in a state of total ignorance. Theory, previous findings, or simple common sense often give us a reasonable expectation of where θtrue might lie — or at least where it almost certainly does not. For instance, if we want to know the average height of all humans on Earth, the value can't possibly be less than 0cm, and it is very unlikely to be above 200cm. Thus, incorporating prior knowledge can meaningfully improve our statistical reasoning.

Bayesian inference allows us to incorporate such prior knowledge. To do this, Bayesians assert that θ is not fixed, but random - i.e., it is not a fixed value at all, but something that can take a range of plausible values. Rather than letting the data speak alone, it combines two sources of information:

  • The likelihood: what does the data tell us about the plausible values of θtrue?
  • The prior: from your prior knowledge, what are the plausible values of θtrue?

The result is the posterior — the updated plausible values of θ after the experiment. Formally:

p(θ | data) ∝ p(data | θ) × p(θ)
posterior ∝ likelihood (from data) × prior (your belief)

With a normal prior N(θ₀, σ₀²) and normal likelihood N(θ̂, σ²), the posterior is also normal. The posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and data mean — the sharper distribution pulls the posterior toward it. The 95% HDI is the shortest interval containing 95% of the posterior probability.
