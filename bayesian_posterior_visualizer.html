What is Bayesian inference?

Combining prior knowledge with observed data

Learning objectives
  • Understand why frequentist inference alone is insufficient when prior knowledge exists
  • Distinguish the likelihood, prior, and posterior distributions
  • Interpret Bayes’ theorem as a principled way to update beliefs with data
  • Understand how the strength of the prior relative to the data shapes the posterior

In the frequentist section, we saw that inference is built entirely from the data — the sampling distribution is centred on our estimate, and all uncertainty is derived from hypothetical replications. Bayesian inference takes a different stance: rather than treating θtrue as an unknown fixed value, it treats it as a random variable with a probability distribution. This allows us to incorporate prior knowledge directly into our analysis.

The result is Bayes’ theorem, which combines two sources of information — what the data says (the likelihood), and what we believed before the data (the prior) — to produce an updated distribution called the posterior:

p(θ | data) ∝ p(data | θ) × p(θ)
posterior ∝ likelihood (from data) × prior (your belief)

The likelihood describes how plausible each candidate value of θ is, given the data you observed. For every possible θ, it asks: how probable was the data I actually collected, if that θ were the true value?

For frequentists, the hard part about the likelihood is that θtrue is assumed to be fixed, so reasoning about “candidate values of θtrue” is illogical. Conventionally, frequentists calculate only the point estimate — the maximum-likelihood estimate θMLE — and discard the remaining candidate values. They then construct a sampling distribution around this θMLE to do their statistical reasoning.

Because Bayesians assume that θtrue itself is a random variable, they are instead able to work with the likelihood’s full range of candidate values directly*. This difference is more philosophical than mathematical. In this demo’s special case (a normal distribution with known variance), the sampling distribution centred at θ̂ is identical to the likelihood**. Frequentists and Bayesians can thus be seen as interpreting the same object through different philosophical lenses.***

* More accurately, Bayesians reject the notion that a sampling distribution is sound at all — since there is only one reality and one experiment, the idea of “hypothetical replications” is too metaphysical.
** The sampling distribution and likelihood are not always identical, but for the location-scale families commonly used in the social sciences (e.g. the t-distribution), they coincide exactly or asymptotically.
*** This workshop aims to be philosophically neutral. Both views are defensible, and the debate remains open.

The prior encodes your belief about θ before seeing any data. It is a full probability distribution: you assign higher probability to values of θ you consider more plausible, and lower (or zero) probability to those you consider unlikely.

In practice, Bayesians typically specify a distributional shape for the prior — such as a normal, t-distribution, or half-Cauchy — and choose its parameters to reflect their beliefs. A wide prior spreads probability across a broad range of θ values, expressing near-ignorance. A narrow prior means you are very certain θ lies in a particular range.

As you will see in the demo below, when the prior is very wide it contributes almost nothing and the posterior converges on the likelihood alone — recovering the frequentist result almost exactly. The frequentist framework can therefore be treated as a special case of the Bayesian framework with a flat (uninformative) prior.

The posterior is the result of combining the two: the likelihood re-weights the prior, pulling it toward the values of θ supported by the data. It is your updated belief about θ after the experiment.

Unlike a frequentist analysis — which gives you a point estimate and a p-value — the posterior is a much richer object. From it you can extract:

  • Point estimate: the posterior mean E[θ], or the MAP (Maximum A Posteriori) — the single peak of the posterior.
  • Credible intervals: a direct probability statement that θ lies within a given range. The 95% HDI (Highest Density Interval) is the shortest interval containing 95% of the posterior probability.
  • Posterior tail probability: the probability that θ exceeds any threshold of interest — the Bayesian analogue of a p-value.

With a normal prior N(θ₀, σ₀²) and normal likelihood N(θ̂, σ²), the posterior is also normal. The posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and data mean — the sharper distribution pulls the posterior toward it. The 95% HDI is the shortest interval containing 95% of the posterior probability.
