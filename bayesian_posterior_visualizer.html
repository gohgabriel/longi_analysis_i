What is Bayesian inference?

Combining both the data and prior knowledge

Learning objectives
  • Understand why frequentist inference alone is insufficient when prior knowledge exists
  • Distinguish the likelihood, prior, and posterior distributions
  • Interpret Bayes’ theorem as a principled way to update beliefs with data
  • Understand how the strength of the prior relative to the data shapes the posterior

Why do Bayesian statistics?

In the frequentist section, we saw that while θtrue is (assumed to be) fixed, we can still construct and approximate a sampling distribution around it to perform statistical inference. However, this is a purely data-driven approach — the plausible values of θ are derived entirely from your data.

In practice, we rarely arrive at a study in a state of total ignorance. Theory, previous findings, or simple common sense often give us a reasonable expectation of where θtrue might lie — or at least where it almost certainly does not. For instance, if we want to know the average height of all humans on Earth, the value can't possibly be less than 0cm, and it is very unlikely to be above 200cm. Thus, incorporating prior knowledge can meaningfully improve our statistical reasoning.

Bayesian inference allows us to incorporate such prior knowledge. To do this, Bayesians assert that θ is not fixed, but random — i.e., it is not a fixed value at all, but something that can take a range of plausible values. Rather than letting the data speak alone, it combines two sources of information:

  • The likelihood: what does the data tell us about the plausible values of θtrue?
  • The prior: from your prior knowledge, what are the plausible values of θtrue?

The result is the posterior — the updated plausible values of θ after the experiment. Formally:

p(θ | data) ∝ p(data | θ) × p(θ)
posterior ∝ likelihood (from data) × prior (your belief)

The likelihood describes how plausible each candidate value of θ is, given the data you observed. For every possible θ, it asks: how probable was the data I actually collected, if that θ were the true value?

For frequentists, the hard part about the likelihood is that θtrue is assumed to be fixed, so reasoning about “candidate values of θtrue” is illogical. Conventionally, frequentists calculate only the point estimate — the maximum-likelihood estimate θMLE — and discard the remaining candidate values. They then construct a sampling distribution around this θMLE to do their statistical reasoning.

Because Bayesians assume that θtrue itself is a random variable that can be described by a probability distribution, they are instead able to work with the likelihood’s candidate θtrue values directly*. This difference is more a philosophical one than a mathematical one. For instance, in this demo’s special case (a normal distribution with known variance), the sampling distribution with its peak at θ̂ is identical to the likelihood**. Thus, frequentists and Bayesians can be seen as just interpreting the same object using a different philosophical lens.***

* More accurately, Bayesians reject the notion that a sampling distribution is sound at all. Bayesians argue that since there is only ever one reality and one experiment conducted, the idea of hypothetical replications from “parallel universes” is too metaphysical to serve as a coherent foundation for probability.
** You can think of the likelihood as a mathematically transformed sampling distribution, and vice versa. The sampling distribution and likelihood are not always identical, but for the location-scale families commonly used in psychology (e.g. the t-distribution), they coincide (exactly or asymptotically) so this intuition remains helpful.
*** This workshop aims to be philosophically neutral. Some textbooks treat the likelihood and sampling distribution as conceptually distinct objects that happen to coincide numerically in simple cases, while others argue the distinction is more philosophical than mathematical. Both views are defensible, and the debate remains open (although the author leans towards the second).

The prior encodes your belief about θ before seeing any data. It is a full probability distribution: you assign higher probability to values of θ you consider more plausible, and lower (or zero) probability to those you consider unlikely. In practice, while we can specify all kinds of different priors, it can be very tedious to do so — because specifying the exact probability you assign to every possible value of θ is practically impossible. As a mathematical shortcut, Bayesians typically specify a distributional shape for the prior, such as a normal, t-distribution, or half-Cauchy (for positive-only values), and then choose its parameters to reflect their beliefs.

Priors are chosen to reflect genuine domain knowledge, such as theory, previous studies, or simple constraints like knowing an effect must be positive. A wide prior spreads probability across a broad range of θ values, expressing near-ignorance. A narrow prior means you are very certain θ lies in a particular range.

As you will see in the demo below, when the prior is very wide, it contributes almost nothing and the posterior converges on the likelihood alone. This recovers the frequentist result almost exactly — meaning you can treat the frequentist framework as a special case of the Bayesian framework.

The posterior is the result of combining the two: the likelihood re-weights the prior, pulling it toward the values of θ supported by the data. It is your updated belief about θ after the experiment.

Unlike a frequentist analysis — which gives you a point estimate and a p-value — the posterior is a much richer object from which you can extract all of the following, and more:

  • Point estimate: the posterior mean (E[θ]), or alternatively the MAP (Maximum A Posteriori) — the single peak value of the posterior distribution.
  • Credible intervals: a direct probability statement that θ lies within a given range. The most common summary is the 95% HDI (Highest Density Interval) — the shortest interval containing 95% of the posterior probability. For a symmetric normal posterior, this is numerically equivalent to the frequentist 95% confidence interval.
  • Posterior tail probability: the probability that θ is greater than, less than, or as extreme as any threshold of interest — the Bayesian analogue of a p-value.

With a normal prior N(θ₀, σ₀²) and normal likelihood N(θ̂, σ²), the posterior is also normal. The posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and data mean — the sharper (more certain) distribution pulls the posterior toward it. The 95% HDI is the shortest interval containing 95% of the posterior probability.
