1.1 What is Longitudinal Data Analysis?

Repeated measures, and why they need special treatment

Learning objectives
  • Understand what distinguishes longitudinal data from cross-sectional data
  • Recognise the two sources of variation unique to repeated-measures designs
  • Know the foundational texts in this field and what each offers
Cross-sectional vs. longitudinal A cross-sectional study measures each person once. A longitudinal study measures the same people repeatedly. This fundamentally changes what you can and cannot claim from your data.
Within vs. between Between-person variation is what differs across individuals — some children are just higher scorers than others. Within-person variation is what changes within the same individual over time. Longitudinal data gives you both. Cross-sectional data gives you only the first.
Why so many methods? GLS, multilevel models, repeated-measures ANOVA, sandwich estimators, hierarchical Bayes are all attempts to solve the same underlying problem. They differ in which assumptions they relax and what they do with within-person information. A unified framework is how we make sense of them all.
Where to go deeper These notes cover the core ideas. If you want to go deeper on any single method, the texts below are where the field goes more in-depth!

Most of what you learned in an introductory statistics course assumes that each observation in your dataset is independent — one person, one row, one data point. That assumption powers everything from the standard error formula to the p-value. It is also, very often, wrong.

In program evaluation, we frequently want to know not just whether a group differs from another group, but whether people change — and whether the program caused that change. To answer that, we measure the same people more than once. That is longitudinal data: observations nested within individuals, collected across time.

The moment you do this, the independence assumption breaks down by design. Two observations from the same child are not independent — they share that child's history, temperament, home environment, and everything else that makes them who they are. Treating them as if they came from different people is not just technically wrong; it discards the most valuable feature of the data.

Two sources of variation

Cross-sectional data has one source of variation: people differ from each other. Longitudinal data has two:

  • Between-person variation — some children simply score higher than others, regardless of the program. This is stable, individual-level signal.
  • Within-person variation — the same child's score changes over time. This is where the program effect lives.

Standard regression conflates these two sources. It sees a dataset with children's scores at different time points and treats all the variation as one undifferentiated pool. When it estimates how scores change over time, it cannot tell whether it is picking up genuine within-person change or just sampling from a mix of high-scoring and low-scoring children at different time points.

Longitudinal methods exist to separate these sources — to give each its proper accounting, and to make sure the program effect estimate is grounded in the right kind of variation.

A landscape of methods

If you have read papers in developmental psychology, education research, or health sciences, you will have encountered a bewildering variety of approaches, such as repeated-measures ANOVA, mixed models, GLS, hierarchical linear models, Bayesian multilevel models, sandwich estimators. Each has its own notation, its own software defaults, its own set of assumptions buried in a methods section.

This proliferation is not arbitrary! Each method is a different answer to a real question about how to handle the correlation structure in longitudinal data, designed by different people for very different contexts. But without a common language for comparing them, it is hard to know why you would choose one over another, or what you are actually assuming when you run a given model.

These notes work through the main methods in this landscape. The goal of this workshop is to help the reader gain genuine understanding of the methods. Ideally, by the end, you should be able to pick up a methods paper and form a view on whether the authors made the right call.

Key readings

These notes are self-contained, but they sit within a broader literature. The following texts are the field's standard references, and each rewards close reading.

Harrell, F. E. (2015). Regression Modeling Strategies (2nd ed.). Springer.
The foundational text on applied regression. Harrell's emphasis on pre-specification, honest model validation, and the costs of data dredging sets the intellectual standard for everything in this course. Particularly relevant: chapters on model assumptions, variable selection, and the relationship between sample size and model complexity.
Fitzmaurice, G. M., Laird, N. M., & Ware, J. H. (2011). Applied Longitudinal Analysis (2nd ed.). Wiley.
The canonical longitudinal-specific textbook. Covers marginal models (GLS), random effects models, and missing data in depth. More mathematically demanding than these notes, but the go-to reference for anyone who wants to understand the theory behind the methods.
Singer, J. D., & Willett, J. B. (2003). Applied Longitudinal Data Analysis. Oxford University Press.
The most accessible entry point in the literature. Singer and Willett's person-period framing — treating each observation as a row, nested within persons — is intuitive and maps cleanly onto modern software. Heavy use of worked examples throughout.
Gelman, A., & Hill, J. (2007). Data Analysis Using Regression and Multilevel/Hierarchical Models. Cambridge University Press.
The bridge between classical and Bayesian multilevel approaches. Gelman and Hill are unusually clear on the conceptual meaning of random effects and partial pooling — ideas that become central in chapters 2.4 and 3.2 of these notes.

Why a framework?

Before working through any individual method, it is worth asking: what are all these methods actually doing? They each have different equations, different assumptions, different outputs — but underneath, they are all solving the same kind of problem. They all make assumptions about how data is generated, choose something to estimate, and use some rule to extract that estimate from the data.

If you can see that structure clearly, a few things become possible. You can compare methods on equal footing. You can identify exactly which assumption a new method is relaxing. You can look at an unfamiliar model and ask the right questions about it, rather than feeling lost in notation.

That is what the next section provides: a five-component framework — Architecture, Parameters, Criterion, Estimator, Solver — that applies equally to every method in this course. It is worth understanding well, because it will be used throughout.
